How to Dispose of a Mattress in Denver (Legally & Cheaply)

Junk RemovalJune 2, 2026

If you've ever tried to get rid of a mattress in Denver, you've probably discovered that it's not as simple as dragging it to the curb. Denver enforces illegal dumping ordinances, and leaving a mattress in an alley or next to a dumpster without scheduling a proper pickup can result in a fine of up to $999. Here's a clear breakdown of every legal option available to Denver-area residents — ranked from cheapest to most convenient.

Why You Can't Just Throw a Mattress Away

Standard curbside trash service in Denver won't pick up a mattress without a special request. Most apartment complexes prohibit mattresses in dumpsters outright. Even if your neighbors have done it, the risk of fines and the environmental harm of landfilling a mattress — which can take decades to break down — make it worth doing right.

Colorado has no statewide mattress recycling mandate, but roughly 75–80% of a mattress is recyclable: the steel springs, polyurethane foam, wood frame, and fabric can all be recovered and reused. That's good news if you care about keeping material out of a landfill.

Your Legal Options in Denver

Option 1: City Bulk Item Pickup (Free, Slow)

Denver residents serviced by Solid Waste Management can schedule a bulk item pickup through Denver's 311 system or the PocketGov app. Fees may apply depending on your service area and item count. The main downside is wait time — you're typically looking at one to two weeks before pickup, and the mattress will need to sit at a designated spot until that day arrives. This works well if you're not in a hurry and the mattress is easily accessible from curbside.

Option 2: Drop-Off at a Recycling Facility

Spring Back Colorado is a nonprofit mattress recycler based in Commerce City that accepts mattresses and box springs. They disassemble each unit and recycle the components, diverting the vast majority from landfill. Drop-off fees apply, and you'll need a truck or trailer to transport the mattress yourself — but it's one of the most environmentally responsible options available locally.

Option 3: Donation (If It Qualifies)

A small number of organizations — ARC Thrift Stores and Habitat for Humanity ReStore — occasionally accept mattresses in near-perfect condition: no stains, no odors, no tears, from a smoke-free and pet-free home. Realistically, the majority of used mattresses don't qualify. Call ahead before loading it up for a trip that might end in rejection.

Option 4: Professional Pickup (Fastest, Most Convenient)

If you want the mattress gone today — without renting a truck, hauling it yourself, or waiting weeks — a junk removal service is the most practical option. A professional crew will carry it from wherever it's sitting (bedroom, basement, third-floor apartment) and route it to a recycling facility or responsible disposal site.

What's Actually Inside a Mattress?

Understanding what you're recycling can make the process feel less abstract:

  • Steel coils/springs — recyclable as scrap metal
  • Polyurethane foam — can be shredded and reused as carpet padding or insulation
  • Wood frame — can be chipped for mulch or biomass fuel
  • Fabric/fiber cover — industrial recycling applications

When mattresses end up in a landfill, these materials are essentially wasted, and the bulky volume they consume is disproportionate to their weight.

Tips for a Smooth Removal

  • Measure doorways beforehand. A queen or king mattress can be surprisingly difficult to maneuver through hallways or around stairwell corners.
  • Don't bag it in plastic unless specifically instructed — it can actually make recycling harder.
  • For bed bug situations, let the removal company know in advance. The mattress should be treated before pickup.
  • Bundle with other items. If you're also clearing out a box spring, bed frame, or other furniture, combining them in a single pickup often makes financial sense.
