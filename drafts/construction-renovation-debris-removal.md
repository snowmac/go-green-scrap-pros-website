A kitchen remodel, bathroom gut, or basement finish always generates more debris than you expect. Drywall scraps, old lumber, tile, concrete, and packaging pile up fast — and most of it can't go in a standard residential trash bin. Understanding your disposal options before demo day saves you from scrambling mid-project.

## What Counts as Construction Debris?

Construction and demolition (C&D) debris covers any material generated during building, renovation, or teardown work. Common categories include:

- **Drywall and plaster** — heavy and bulky; most regular haulers won't touch it
- **Lumber and framing materials** — dimensional lumber, old trim, subflooring
- **Concrete, brick, and tile** — extremely heavy; weight limits matter here
- **Roofing materials** — shingles, felt, decking boards
- **Metal** — old pipes, duct work, wire, metal framing
- **Flooring** — carpet, hardwood, laminate, vinyl, tile
- **Windows and doors** — glass and frame material from replacements
- **Insulation** — fiberglass batts and rigid foam

Each material type has different disposal considerations, and some — like certain types of insulation or older homes with potential lead paint — require extra care.

## What You Can't Put in the Regular Trash (or a Rented Dumpster)

Most residential trash pickup won't accept construction debris regardless of volume. Even if you rent a dumpster for a remodel, most dumpster rental contracts exclude:

- Concrete and masonry (weight restrictions)
- Asbestos-containing materials (requires licensed abatement)
- Lead-painted materials from older homes (may require special handling)
- Hazardous chemicals (paints, solvents, adhesives — check with the rental company)

Always read the dumpster rental agreement. Overweight or prohibited material loads result in additional fees that can surprise you significantly.

## Denver's Construction Debris Rules

For permitted commercial and larger residential projects in Denver, the city has adopted construction and demolition waste diversion requirements. As of ordinances currently moving through Denver's regulatory process, contractors on qualifying projects are expected to divert a significant portion of C&D debris through recycling and reuse rather than landfill disposal — at least 50% for permits issued on or after September 2026, with higher targets phased in over time.

For typical homeowner renovations — a bathroom remodel, deck replacement, or garage conversion — these commercial-scale requirements generally don't apply. But the principle is still useful: many C&D materials can be recycled or diverted if you plan for it.

## Recycling Options for Renovation Debris

Not all construction waste belongs in a landfill:

### Metal
Old plumbing pipes, metal duct work, copper wire, steel framing, and metal fixtures all have scrap value. Separating metal from the debris pile can offset disposal costs — and in some cases, the metal is worth picking up for free.

Our [scrap metal pickup](/scrap-metal-pickup/) service handles metals from renovation projects. Pull your copper, aluminum, and steel pipe and we'll take care of it.

### Lumber
Clean, unpainted dimensional lumber can sometimes be donated through Habitat for Humanity ReStore locations in the Denver area. They accept usable building materials for resale to fund their homebuilding programs.

### Concrete and Masonry
Concrete is heavy and requires specialized handling, but it can be recycled as aggregate. Some Colorado aggregate recyclers accept clean concrete. Call ahead to confirm current acceptance and get weight restrictions.

### Drywall
New, uncontaminated drywall scraps can sometimes be recycled at specialty facilities. Used drywall is more complicated due to potential contamination. Check with local recyclers before assuming it can be diverted.

## Renting a Dumpster vs. Hiring a Hauler

For large renovation projects, a temporary dumpster rental gives you a place to throw debris continuously throughout the job. Advantages:

- Convenience — debris goes in as work happens
- Good for projects spanning multiple days or weeks
- Your crew controls the pace

Disadvantages:

- Dumpsters sit in your driveway and may need a permit if placed on the street
- Prohibited material rules limit what can go in
- Weight overages can be expensive

For smaller projects — a bathroom remodel, single-room renovation, or cleanup after contractors leave — a single haul-away pickup is often more efficient. We can take mixed loads of renovation debris and sort recyclable metal from general disposal material.
