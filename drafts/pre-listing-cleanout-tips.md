Listing photos are the first thing buyers see. In Denver's competitive market, a home that photographs clean and open gets more showings than one cluttered with leftover furniture and garage junk — even if the underlying property is identical. A pre-listing cleanout is one of the highest-return things you can do before your home hits the market.

Here's a practical guide to what to clear, how to prioritize, and how to get it done before your photographer shows up.

## Why Pre-Listing Cleanouts Matter in Denver

Denver buyers scroll through dozens of listings online before deciding which homes to visit. Wide-angle real estate photography makes cluttered spaces look worse than they are, and cramped photos drive buyers away before they even read the description.

A clean, lightly furnished space does several things:

- Makes every room appear larger than a cluttered version of the same room
- Lets buyers visualize their own belongings in the space
- Signals that the home has been well maintained
- Gives photographers and stagers room to work

The fastest way to improve how your home shows is to remove things, not add them.

## What to Clear Out: Room by Room

### Main Living Areas

- Excess furniture — edit down to one seating area; remove extra chairs, side tables, oversized sectionals
- Personal collections, family photos, and anything that screams "someone else's house"
- Old electronics (tube TVs, printers, outdated gear)
- Area rugs that visually shrink the space

### Kitchen

- Clear countertops completely — a coffee maker and a bowl of fruit is enough
- Remove small appliances stored on counters
- Clear out cluttered pantries and cabinets (buyers open them)
- Dispose of old cleaning supplies under the sink

### Bedrooms and Closets

- Remove excess furniture; a bed, dresser, and nightstand is sufficient for photos
- Closets should look 30–50% empty — this signals ample storage to buyers
- Clear under-bed storage
- Pack away off-season clothing to make closets feel spacious

### Garage

The garage is frequently the last thing sellers tackle and the one that most needs attention. Denver buyers look at garages carefully — they want to see floor space, not a wall of accumulated stuff.

- Remove everything not being sold with the home
- Pull old tools, sports gear, and seasonal items
- Separate scrap metal — old shelving, bikes, tools — for recycling or free pickup
- Old paint cans require proper disposal (Colorado's PaintCare program has drop-off locations throughout the metro)

### Basement

Buyers want to see usable square footage. A basement packed with boxes signals storage overflow, not value.

- Clear stored boxes and furniture
- Remove exercise equipment that won't convey with the sale
- Clean out old water heaters, furnace parts, and utility room clutter

### Exterior and Yard

Curb appeal sets the tone. Before listing photos:

- Remove dead plants, overgrown brush, and yard debris
- Clear old patio furniture that's faded or broken
- Take out old grills, planters, and anything making the yard look neglected
- Clean up garage or shed exteriors

## Sequence Matters: Junk Removal Before Everything Else

The common mistake is scheduling listing photos too early. The right sequence:

1. **Clear every room and make decisions** about what stays and what goes
2. **Schedule junk removal** to take out everything you're not keeping or donating
3. **Deep clean** after items are removed (cleaning around furniture doesn't count)
4. **Touch-up paint** where needed — walls behind heavy furniture, nail holes
5. **Stage key rooms** with what's staying
6. **Schedule listing photos** last

Skipping steps 2 or 3 leaves stagers and photographers working around your stuff instead of your home's best features.

## What to Do With Items You're Removing

### Donate

Items in good condition can go to ARC Thrift Stores (which offers free furniture pickup along the Front Range), Goodwill of Colorado, Habitat for Humanity ReStore, and similar organizations. Get these scheduled early — pickup availability varies.

### Sell

High-value items that didn't make the staging cut can be listed on Facebook Marketplace before or during the listing process. Buyers typically handle pickup.

## Work With Your Realtor

Your agent has seen hundreds of homes hit the market. Ask them to walk the property with fresh eyes and flag exactly which items need to go. Most will be honest — they want the listing to photograph well.

Realtors often have preferred vendors for junk removal and staging. If your agent recommends a timeline, work backward from listing day and schedule your cleanout first.
