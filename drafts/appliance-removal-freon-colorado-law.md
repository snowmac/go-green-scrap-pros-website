Getting rid of an old refrigerator, window air conditioner, or dehumidifier involves more than just hauling it to the curb. These appliances contain refrigerants — commonly called "Freon," though that's a brand name covering several chemical compounds — that are regulated under federal environmental law. Understanding the basics protects you from inadvertent violations and ensures the job gets done right.

## Why Refrigerant Matters

Refrigerants used in older appliances include compounds that contribute to ozone depletion and climate change when released into the atmosphere. Federal regulations under EPA Section 608 prohibit the intentional venting of these substances. That rule applies to anyone handling refrigerant, including homeowners, haulers, and scrap dealers.

The practical implication: you can't legally drop off a refrigerator at a scrap yard or landfill without first certifying that the refrigerant has been removed. Most reputable facilities require written documentation confirming proper refrigerant recovery before they'll accept the appliance.

## Which Appliances Are Affected?

Any appliance with a sealed refrigeration system is subject to these rules, including:

- Refrigerators and freezers (full-size and mini-fridges)
- Window and portable air conditioners
- Central AC units and heat pumps
- Dehumidifiers
- Wine coolers and beverage refrigerators
- Old water coolers with refrigeration

Clothes washers, dryers, and dishwashers do not contain refrigerant and are not subject to these rules — they can be disposed of more straightforwardly (though they still require proper appliance recycling or scrap handling).

## What Proper Refrigerant Removal Looks Like

Refrigerant must be recovered by a technician using EPA-certified recovery equipment before the appliance can be scrapped or discarded. This is not a DIY process — the equipment and certification requirements make it a job for licensed professionals.

When you use a responsible appliance removal company, refrigerant recovery is handled as part of the service. The technician documents the removal, and that paperwork follows the appliance through the disposal chain.

### What to ask any removal company

Before hiring anyone to haul your appliance:

- Do you handle refrigerant recovery, or do you subcontract it?
- Will you provide documentation that the refrigerant was properly recovered?
- Are the technicians handling refrigerant EPA-certified?

A company that can't answer these questions confidently is worth avoiding.

## Colorado-Specific Context

Colorado's regulations align with federal EPA requirements. Appliances dropped at Front Range transfer stations and landfills must arrive with documentation of refrigerant removal, or the facility must perform recovery on-site. Most municipal facilities serving Adams, Jefferson, Arapahoe, and Boulder counties have processes in place — but they charge for this service, and they will turn away appliances that arrive without proper paperwork.

Utility programs occasionally offer rebates or free pickup for certain appliances (particularly older, inefficient refrigerators and freezers). Xcel Energy has offered such programs periodically for its Colorado customers — check their website for current availability before scheduling a haul.

## Refrigerator vs. Air Conditioner: Any Difference?

The rules are similar, but the recovery process and refrigerant types differ:

- **Refrigerators and freezers** typically contain smaller refrigerant charges and are handled routinely by appliance recyclers.
- **Window AC units** and **portable ACs** also require recovery but are often smaller charges.
- **Central AC and heat pump systems** contain larger refrigerant quantities and are almost always handled by HVAC contractors rather than junk removal companies.

For central AC removal, coordinate with a licensed HVAC contractor for refrigerant recovery first, then schedule a hauler for the physical unit removal.
