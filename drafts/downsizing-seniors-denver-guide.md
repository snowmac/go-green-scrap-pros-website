Downsizing is one of the most emotionally complex moves a person can make. Whether you're a senior preparing to move into a smaller home, a condo, or an assisted living community — or a family member helping a parent through this transition — the process involves far more than packing boxes. It means making decisions about a lifetime of accumulated belongings, often under time pressure and emotional strain.

This guide is designed to help Denver-area families approach senior downsizing with clarity, compassion, and a practical plan.

## Start Earlier Than You Think You Need To

The most consistent advice from people who have been through a senior move: start earlier. Three to six months before the move date is not excessive, especially for a home with decades of belongings. The process of going through a lifetime of accumulated items — deciding what comes along, what goes to family, what gets donated, and what gets hauled — takes far longer than most people estimate.

If the move is being compressed by a health event, a facility transition, or a sale closing date, prioritize ruthlessly and call in help rather than trying to do everything yourself.

## Step 1: Right-Size for the New Space

Before you can decide what to keep, you need to understand what the new space can hold. Get the floor plan of the new home or unit as early as possible. Measure key pieces of furniture and compare them to room dimensions. Seniors moving from a 2,000-square-foot home to a 700-square-foot apartment will need to make hard choices — it's easier to make them on paper before they're physical reality.

Key questions:
- How many bedrooms and how much storage does the new place have?
- Is there a preference for familiar furniture, or a fresh start with new pieces?
- What are the building's policies on move-in procedures, elevator access, and move-in hours?

## Step 2: Categorize Belongings Before Moving Anything

Establish clear categories for every item in the home:

1. **Moving** — comes to the new place
2. **Family claim** — designated for specific family members
3. **Sell** — items with resale value worth marketing
4. **Donate** — functional items that charities can use
5. **Recycle** — metal, electronics, and other recyclable materials
6. **Haul/dispose** — worn or broken items with no other path

Work room by room with a notebook or a simple spreadsheet. Don't try to hold these decisions in your head — the volume of items is too large.

## Step 3: Handling Heirlooms and Sentimental Items

These are the decisions that take the longest and carry the most emotional weight. A few approaches that help:

- **Photograph everything** with sentimental value, even items that can't be kept. A digital record preserves the memory even when the object has to go.
- **Don't force decisions on a timeline that doesn't respect the emotion involved.** It's okay to have a "decide later" box for a small number of items.
- **Involve family members early** — adult children and grandchildren may want items that a senior wouldn't expect. A quick email or group text with photos can surface interest quickly.
- **Consider a small estate sale** for items with meaningful antique or collectible value. Local estate sale companies handle pricing and buyer management.

## Donation Options in the Denver Area

For functional furniture, clothing, and household goods:

- **ARC Thrift Stores** — furniture, clothing, and household items
- **Habitat for Humanity ReStore** — furniture, building materials, and appliances in good condition
- **Denver Furniture Bank** — provides furniture to families in need; contact them directly about donation eligibility
- **Goodwill** — general goods, clothing, and smaller household items

A few things most donation centers won't accept: mattresses (unless near-perfect), heavily worn furniture, or electronics in poor condition. Call ahead for large loads.

## Managing Large Furniture and Appliances

Large pieces — bedroom sets, china cabinets, entertainment centers, pianos, and appliances — require special handling. Options include:

- **Family pickup** — coordinate with family before the move date for any large claimed items
- **Facebook Marketplace** — larger furniture pieces often move quickly on local platforms
- **Professional junk removal** — for pieces that can't be donated or don't sell, a professional crew handles the heavy lifting and responsible disposal

## The Emotional Side of This Work

For seniors moving from a longtime home, every item carries memory. A patient, non-judgmental approach matters. The goal is to help — not to rush through decisions or make someone feel that their belongings don't matter. If you're the family member coordinating the move, give yourself permission to grieve the transition too. It's a big life change.
