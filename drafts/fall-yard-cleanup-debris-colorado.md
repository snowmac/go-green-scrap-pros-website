Fall in Colorado moves fast. One week you're looking at golden aspens; the next, a surprise October snowstorm has knocked branches off your elms and left you staring at a yard's worth of debris to deal with before the ground freezes solid. If you're a Denver-area homeowner, getting ahead of fall yard cleanup before Thanksgiving is the right call — both for the health of your lawn and to avoid dealing with frozen, compacted debris come spring.

Here's a practical guide to what needs doing and how to handle the things that regular yard waste pickup won't take.

## What Colorado Fall Cleanup Actually Involves

Fall yard cleanup in the Denver metro isn't just raking leaves. Depending on your property, it can include:

- **Leaf collection and removal** — particularly heavy in neighborhoods with mature cottonwoods, elms, and oaks
- **Tree branch and limb debris** — early snow and wind storms from September through November routinely bring down branches
- **Dead annuals and garden beds** — pulling and clearing summer plants before frost locks them in
- **Lawn aeration and winterization** — best done in fall, especially for cool-season grasses common at Colorado's elevation
- **Gutter clearing** — critical before freeze-thaw cycles start expanding debris into ice dams
- **Clearing out sheds and outdoor storage** — seasonal equipment swap (lawn gear out, snow gear in)
- **Outdoor furniture** — storing or covering for winter

## Handling Yard Waste in the Denver Metro

Most Denver metro cities collect yard waste through green carts or seasonal pickup programs. Denver's composting program accepts grass, leaves, and small branches. However, there are important limitations:

- **Branches and limbs over certain size thresholds** are not accepted in standard green carts. Denver's guidelines specify limits on branch diameter and length — check your city's solid waste site for current specs.
- **Lumber, treated wood, stumps, and large root balls** are not accepted as yard waste.
- **Mixed debris** (yard waste combined with household junk) gets rejected.

Large branch piles, tree stumps, and significant storm cleanup debris require separate handling — either through a scheduled bulk pickup or a debris removal service.

### Cities with Notable Yard Waste Programs

- **Denver** — composting collection year-round; green cart program
- **Thornton** — seasonal yard waste collection; check city schedule
- **Arvada** — yard waste collection available; bulk pickup for large loads
- **Westminster** — seasonal composting program
- **Boulder** — Eco-Cycle composting; strong programs across the city

Call 311 or check your city's website for current seasonal schedules, as pickup dates shift in fall.

## The Storm Damage Problem

Colorado's fall shoulder season — especially September and October — brings snowstorms that can arrive before leaves have dropped. Heavy, wet snow loading onto fully-leafed branches causes significant breakage. After a storm event, you may find:

- Large limbs or entire tree sections down in the yard
- Fence damage from fallen branches
- Debris scattered across the property

Standard yard waste pickup programs are not equipped for large-scale storm debris. If you're looking at a significant branch pile or structural debris from storm damage, a dedicated debris removal call is often the most efficient path.

## What to Do with Items That Aren't Yard Waste

Fall is also the season when homeowners clear out sheds, garages, and outdoor storage areas — which means yard cleanup often turns into a general junk removal project. Common fall discoveries include:

- **Old outdoor furniture** — rusted patio chairs, broken tables, worn deck furniture
- **Defunct outdoor equipment** — non-running lawn mowers, tillers, leaf blowers, edgers
- **Old grills** — propane grills have scrap metal value in the grate and body; remove and properly dispose of propane tanks first
- **Broken fencing materials** — wood fence rails and posts (not accepted in yard waste), metal posts (scrap value)
- **Garden tools that have seen better days**

Metal components from outdoor equipment — lawnmower decks, rototiller frames, steel fence posts — have genuine scrap value and can often be picked up free as part of a scrap metal run.

## Planning Your Fall Cleanup Around Colorado Weather

The best window for fall yard work in the Denver metro is typically late September through mid-November. A few Colorado-specific factors to keep in mind:

- The Front Range gets early snowfall every year — sometimes significant amounts in September. If you're behind on fall cleanup, reprioritize before the first major storm.
- Frozen ground makes post-storm branch cleanup much harder; organic debris compacted under snow and ice is difficult to move and creates problems for lawn health the following spring.
- Wind is a major factor. Denver's famous downslope chinook winds regularly reach 40–60 mph and can scatter debris across the neighborhood.
