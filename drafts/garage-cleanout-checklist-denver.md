A garage cleanout is one of those projects that always seems easier in theory than in practice. You open the door, stare at two decades of accumulated gear, power tools, holiday decorations, and mystery boxes, and promptly close it again. But with a clear system and the right plan for each category of stuff, a Denver-area garage cleanout is very manageable — even satisfying.

This checklist walks you through the whole process from start to finish.

## Before You Start: Set Up a Sorting Zone

You'll need space to make decisions. If your garage is too packed to work in, use the driveway. Pull everything out in sections and create four physical zones:

1. **Keep** — Items returning to the garage that you use regularly
2. **Donate** — Functional items someone else could use
3. **Scrap/Recycle** — Metal, electronics, and other recyclables
4. **Trash/Haul** — Broken, unusable, or non-recyclable items

Work zone by zone rather than all at once. The goal isn't to decide on everything immediately — it's to create an organized structure so you can move through decisions efficiently.

## The Garage Cleanout Checklist

### Zone 1: Tools and Hardware

- [ ] Hand tools — keep what you use, donate duplicates and unused specialty tools
- [ ] Power tools — test each one; non-working tools may have metal scrap value
- [ ] Hardware bins — sort and consolidate screws, bolts, and fasteners; toss rusted or unidentifiable pieces
- [ ] Old paint cans — partially-full cans need special handling (see below)
- [ ] Extension cords — test and keep functional ones, discard frayed or damaged cords

**Paint note:** Denver's hazardous waste program and Colorado's PaintCare program accept leftover paint. Check Denver's household hazardous waste schedule or find a PaintCare drop-off location — many hardware stores participate.

### Zone 2: Sporting and Outdoor Gear

- [ ] Bikes — working bikes donate well; broken frames may have scrap aluminum or steel value
- [ ] Camping gear — tents, sleeping bags, and coolers are often donated or resold
- [ ] Skiing/snowboarding equipment — Colorado resale shops often accept these
- [ ] Lawn and garden tools — donate functional, scrap metal the rest

### Zone 3: Appliances and Large Items

- [ ] Old refrigerators or freezers — require Freon recovery before disposal; [large appliance removal](/large-appliance-removal/) handles this correctly
- [ ] Washing machines, dryers — heavy scrap metal value in the drum and frame
- [ ] Old water heaters — steel and copper content makes these worth scrapping
- [ ] Air conditioners — Freon issues apply here too; don't cut lines or vent to atmosphere

### Zone 4: Furniture, Boxes, and Miscellaneous

- [ ] Old furniture — check donation eligibility; couches, chairs, and shelving often accepted by ARC or Habitat ReStore
- [ ] Storage boxes — sort contents before deciding; many boxes contain forgotten valuables
- [ ] Holiday decorations — donate or keep; discard broken strings of lights responsibly

### Zone 5: Hazardous and Specialty Items

Some items require special disposal and cannot go in regular trash:

- [ ] Motor oil and automotive fluids — AutoZone and O'Reilly Auto Parts locations accept used motor oil
- [ ] Propane tanks — exchange programs at most hardware stores
- [ ] Batteries — car batteries have scrap value; household batteries go to designated drop-offs
- [ ] Fluorescent bulbs — hazardous waste events or participating retailers

## How to Handle Scrap Metal from a Garage Cleanout

A Denver-area garage cleanout often turns up meaningful amounts of metal: old shelving, aluminum ladders, copper wire, cast iron tools, and steel bike frames. Rather than hauling all of it to the trash, sort metal from non-metal items. Copper and brass are especially valuable; steel and aluminum are worth keeping separate for recycling.
