When a loved one passes or moves into care, the task of clearing out their home falls on family members who are often emotionally exhausted, geographically scattered, and uncertain where to even begin. An estate cleanout is not just a physical project — it's a process that requires sensitivity, organization, and a realistic plan.

This guide is for families navigating that process in the Denver metro area. It won't make it easy, but it will make it clearer.

## Step 1: Give Yourself Adequate Time

Rushing an estate cleanout is one of the most common mistakes families make. If there's a hard deadline — a lease end date, a real estate sale closing, a storage unit expiring — work backward from that date and build a realistic schedule. For a furnished home, plan on at least two to four full days of sorting before any hauling begins.

If the property is large or decades of belongings have accumulated, the timeline may need to extend further. This is not a job that benefits from cutting corners on the front end.

## Step 2: Secure Valuables and Important Documents First

Before any sorting or hauling begins, do a full walkthrough specifically looking for:

- Legal documents: wills, deeds, insurance policies, financial statements
- Prescription medications (these require specific disposal)
- Jewelry, cash, coins, and other high-value small items
- Sentimental heirlooms that family members may want
- Safe deposit box keys, computer passwords, or other access credentials

Older homes in particular can hold cash and valuables in unexpected places — inside books, in coat pockets, under furniture, in boxes in the back of closets. Walk the home carefully before assuming anything is trash.

## Step 3: Coordinate with Family Members

If multiple people have a stake in the estate, establish a process before starting. Some approaches that work:

- **Family meeting or video call** to discuss who wants which items before anyone claims anything
- **Round-robin claiming** for items with sentimental value but no clear designated recipient
- **Photo inventory** for out-of-town family who can't be present for the walkthrough
- **Clear communication about what happens to unclaimed items** — donated, sold, or hauled

Disagreements over belongings are common and can derail the process entirely. Establishing a fair system upfront prevents most conflicts.

## Step 4: Categorize Everything Before Moving Anything

The four-category sort works well for estate cleanouts:

1. **Keep** — items being retained by family
2. **Sell or Donate** — functional items with value to others
3. **Recycle** — scrap metal, electronics, and materials with recycling streams
4. **Haul/Dispose** — worn, broken, or unusable items

Work room by room and don't move items from the sort zones until each category is clearly established. Mixing the categories leads to confusion and wasted trips.

## Step 5: Handle Donations Thoughtfully

Denver-area donation options for estate items include:

- **ARC Thrift Stores** — furniture, clothing, housewares, tools
- **Habitat for Humanity ReStore** — building materials, appliances, hardware
- **Denver Furniture Bank** — provides furniture to families transitioning out of homelessness
- **St. Francis Cabrini Thrift Store** — general goods, Westminster area
- **Books** — Better World Books has drop boxes, or donate to Denver Public Library's Friends program

Note that most donation centers will not accept mattresses (unless in near-perfect condition), broken furniture, or heavily worn clothing. Call ahead for large furniture loads.

## Step 6: Identify What Has Scrap or Recycling Value

Estate cleanouts often contain materials that shouldn't go in the trash:

- **Scrap metal** — old tools, aluminum ladders, copper pipe, appliances
- **Electronics** — computers, TVs, and phones are banned from Colorado landfills under state law (SB 12-133)
- **Medications** — many pharmacies and police departments have medication drop-boxes for proper disposal
- **Hazardous materials** — paint, solvents, pesticides — Denver's hazardous waste program handles these

## Step 7: Arrange Hauling for What Remains

Once valuables, donations, and recyclables are sorted out, what's left is the haul load: furniture that didn't sell, worn items, general debris, and anything that can't be donated or recycled.

For a full estate cleanout, a professional junk removal crew is far more efficient than multiple personal truck trips. A team can typically clear an average home's remaining contents in a few hours. For items with scrap metal value, a combined approach — scrap pickup plus junk removal — often reduces or eliminates cost on the metal portion.
