Old televisions are one of the most common items people get stuck on when decluttering. You can't put them in the trash (it's illegal in Colorado), most donation centers don't want them, and they're awkward to move. Whether you've got a 200-pound CRT from the early 2000s or a cracked 65-inch flat screen, here's exactly how to handle TV recycling in Colorado.

## Colorado's TV Disposal Law

Colorado's Electronic Recycling Jobs Act (SB 12-133), which took effect on July 1, 2013, bans televisions from landfill disposal statewide. This applies to all types: CRT (tube-style), LCD, LED, OLED, and plasma. It also applies to computer monitors and a range of other electronics.

Putting a TV in your trash bin is illegal, and waste haulers are prohibited from knowingly collecting them. This applies whether you're a homeowner, renter, or business.

## Recycling Options by TV Type

### Flat-Screen TVs (LCD, LED, OLED, Plasma)

Modern flat screens are the easiest to deal with because they're lighter and more recycling infrastructure exists for them.

**Free and low-cost options:**
- **Best Buy** — accepts flat-screen TVs up to 32 inches free; larger screens (over 32 inches) carry a recycling fee. Limit three items per household per day. No purchase required.
- **Blue Star Recyclers (Denver)** — located in Denver's Sun Valley neighborhood at 953 Decatur St. Denver County residents can request a free E-cycle Coupon from Denver Solid Waste Management to reduce or eliminate the drop-off cost. One coupon per household per year.
- **Sustainability Recycling (Arvada/Westminster/Broomfield)** — accepts flat-screen TVs by the pound; call ahead for current pricing and limits.
- **Eco-Cycle CHaRM (Boulder)** — 6400 Arapahoe Road, accepts televisions along with other electronics.

**Manufacturer take-back programs:**
Samsung, LG, Sony, and other major manufacturers have recycling programs, though programs and requirements change. Check the manufacturer's website directly for current options.

### CRT Televisions (Old Tube-Style Sets)

CRT televisions are the most difficult category because of their weight (a 32-inch CRT can weigh 80–150 pounds) and their hazardous materials content: each unit contains several pounds of lead in the glass funnel. This makes them expensive to recycle — recyclers have to pay to process the lead glass, unlike metals which generate revenue.

**What to know before you try to move one:**
- Get help. These things are genuinely dangerous to move alone — heavy, top-heavy, and awkward to grip.
- Many donation centers refuse CRTs entirely. Don't assume Goodwill or ARC will take it.
- Even Best Buy charges a recycling fee for CRTs regardless of size.

**Where to take a CRT TV:**
- **Blue Star Recyclers** — accepts CRT televisions; Denver residents with the E-cycle coupon get a reduced rate
- **Sustainability Recycling (Arvada/Westminster/Broomfield)** — accepts CRTs at a per-pound rate with quantity limits (10 CRTs per visit at Arvada and Westminster locations)
- **Arapahoe County Hazardous Waste Facility (Englewood)** — free for county residents on event days

### Projection TVs

Rear-projection televisions — those large floor-standing units from the late 1990s and 2000s — present their own challenges. Sustainability Recycling in Arvada and Westminster accepts them at the same per-pound rate as other CRTs, with an additional charge for units with wooden housing.

## Can You Donate a Working TV?

- **Working flat-screen under 10 years old, no cracks** — Goodwill and ARC may accept; call ahead
- **Working CRT** — very few donation centers accept them anymore due to zero resale demand
- **Broken TV of any type** — not accepted anywhere for donation; recycling only

If your flat screen is in good shape, try Facebook Marketplace or Nextdoor first. Working TVs in good condition often sell for $25–$100 locally, depending on size and brand.

## What Happens When a TV Is Recycled?

At a certified e-waste recycling facility, televisions are disassembled and components are separated:

- **Copper, aluminum, and steel** — recovered as scrap metal
- **Plastics** — sorted and processed for reuse where possible
- **CRT glass** — lead is extracted and processed by specialized facilities
- **Circuit boards** — refined to recover gold, silver, and other trace metals

Over 90% of the materials in a modern flat screen can be recovered and returned to productive use.
