Spring in Colorado means mud season, longer days, and the irresistible urge to dig out from under a winter's worth of accumulated stuff. Whether you're doing a light refresh or a full whole-house purge, a room-by-room plan keeps the project from turning into an overwhelming weekend.

Here's a practical framework built for Denver metro homeowners — with honest guidance on what to donate, what to toss, and when to bring in professional help.

## Start With a System, Not a Room

Before you open a single closet, decide how you'll categorize items. The classic three-pile method works:

- **Keep** — you use it, love it, or genuinely need it
- **Donate/Sell** — in good condition, someone else can use it
- **Remove** — broken, expired, unsellable, or just done

Have bags and boxes ready for donate/sell items. For the remove pile, decide early whether you'll haul it yourself or schedule a pickup — having a target date keeps the project moving.

## Room-by-Room Guide

### Kitchen

Start here. Kitchens accumulate duplicates, expired food, and gadgets used twice.

- Clear out expired pantry items and dispose of properly
- Donate appliances you haven't used in a year (blenders, bread makers, coffee gadgets)
- Toss worn or warped cookware, chipped dishes, and mismatched containers
- Clear the junk drawer — truly clear it, not just reorganize it

### Living Room and Dining Room

Focus on furniture scale and hidden clutter.

- Remove any furniture that crowds the space or no longer fits your life
- Sort bookshelves — donate books you won't re-read, recycle damaged paperbacks
- Check under furniture and inside ottomans for items that've migrated there

### Bedrooms and Closets

Apply the one-year rule: if you haven't worn or used it in the past year, it goes.

- Bag clothing by category (donate good condition, toss worn)
- Clear under-bed storage — that's prime space getting wasted
- Tackle the guest room, which often doubles as a storage unit

### Bathrooms

Small space, fast payoff.

- Dispose of expired medications (Denver has several safe medication drop-off locations — check Walgreens and police station drop boxes)
- Toss old cosmetics, dried-out products, and anything you wouldn't reach for in a normal week
- Clear countertops completely

### Garage

The biggest project for most Denver homeowners. Allocate a full day.

- Sort tools: keep what works, donate duplicates, scrap the rusted or broken
- Pull out old bikes, sports gear, and camping equipment — anything that hasn't moved in years
- Separate scrap metal (old shelving, pipes, hardware) — this is often free to haul through our [scrap metal pickup](/scrap-metal-pickup/) service
- Dispose of old paint and chemicals through proper channels (Arapahoe County and Jefferson County both run household hazardous waste collection events)

### Basement and Attic

These rooms become the graveyard for things you didn't want to decide about. Now is the time.

- Open every box. If you don't remember what's in it, you don't need it
- Remove old furniture, exercise equipment, and large items that have been "temporary" for years
- Anything that needs to go down stairs and out the door is a candidate for professional removal

## When to Call for Help

Spring cleaning tips you into professional territory when:

- **Volume** — you've got more than a truckload of stuff
- **Weight** — old furniture, appliances, or exercise equipment you can't safely move alone
- **Time** — you have a weekend, not a month
- **Hazardous items** — old TVs, refrigerators, or anything with freon

## What to Do With Donations

Several organizations in the Denver area accept gently used items:

- **Goodwill of Colorado** — clothing, housewares, furniture, electronics
- **ARC Thrift Stores** — Colorado-based, with free pickup for furniture along the Front Range
- **Habitat for Humanity ReStore** — furniture, appliances, building materials
- **Salvation Army** — furniture pickup scheduling available

Keep your donation receipts. If you itemize deductions, donated goods at fair market value are generally tax-deductible.

## Make It Stick

A spring cleanout only lasts if you don't refill the space. After the purge:

- Apply a one-in-one-out rule for new purchases
- Schedule a smaller quarterly check-in so nothing accumulates to overwhelming levels again
- Use your newly cleared garage or basement for actual intended purposes

Colorado's shoulder seasons — spring and fall — are the best windows to tackle big projects before the heat or cold makes outdoor work miserable.
