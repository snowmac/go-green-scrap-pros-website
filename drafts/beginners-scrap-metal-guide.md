If you've accumulated metal from a renovation, estate cleanout, or garage clear-out and you're wondering whether any of it is worth something — the short answer is probably yes, at least some of it. Scrap metal is bought by weight at licensed scrap yards across the Denver metro, and the value of different metals varies considerably.

This beginner's guide covers the three metals you'll encounter most often as a homeowner: copper, aluminum, and steel. Learn how to identify them, what they're worth, and how to maximize your payout.

## The Most Important Tool: A Magnet

Before anything else, grab a simple refrigerator magnet. This single tool sorts your scrap into two categories:

- **Sticks to the magnet** → Ferrous metal (iron and steel). Lower value per pound, but widely accepted.
- **Doesn't stick** → Non-ferrous metal (copper, aluminum, brass, stainless steel). Higher value, more worth sorting carefully.

Non-ferrous metals are where most of the money is. If you can identify and separate them from your steel pile, you'll get substantially better returns.

## Copper: The Most Valuable Common Scrap Metal

### What It Looks Like

Copper has a distinctive reddish-orange color when clean. Older copper may have a green patina (verdigris) from oxidation. It's significantly heavier than it looks — a short piece of copper pipe is noticeably denser than an equal-length piece of steel pipe.

### Where You Find It

- Electrical wiring (the conductor inside the insulation)
- Plumbing pipe (water supply lines, especially in older homes)
- HVAC refrigerant lines
- Old appliance motors and compressors
- Copper fittings, elbows, and valves

### Grades and Pricing

Scrap yards grade copper based on cleanliness and purity:

- **Bare bright copper wire** — clean, uncoated, unalloyed copper wire stripped of insulation. This earns the highest price.
- **#1 copper** — clean copper pipe and tubing, free of fittings, solder, and paint.
- **#2 copper** — copper with light oxidation, solder, or minor contamination. Slightly lower price.
- **Insulated copper wire** — wire still in its insulation jacket. Priced based on the estimated copper content (recovery rate), much lower than stripped wire.

**What this means for you:** Stripping insulation from electrical wire before taking it to the yard is worth the effort if you have meaningful quantity. Even a few pounds of bare bright copper versus insulated wire can result in a noticeably higher payout.

Copper prices fluctuate with commodity markets. As a general reference, copper has historically been the highest-paying common scrap metal, often several times the price of aluminum per pound. Always call your local yard for today's rate.

## Aluminum: Lightweight and Widely Available

### What It Looks Like

Aluminum is silver-gray and notably lightweight. If you pick up a piece of metal and it's surprisingly light — it's probably aluminum. It won't rust (though it oxidizes to a dull gray), and it won't attract a magnet.

### Where You Find It

- Window frames and storm windows
- Exterior siding
- Patio and lawn furniture
- Ladders
- Automotive wheels and engine components
- Aluminum cans (low payout per pound, but widely accepted)
- Gutters and downspouts

### Grades and Pricing

- **Clean aluminum** (extrusions, sheet, cast pieces free of paint and attachments) earns the most
- **Dirty aluminum** (painted, mixed with other materials, or heavily oxidized) earns less
- **Aluminum cans** are accepted but pay at a lower rate than other aluminum — useful for recycling, but don't expect significant returns from a bag of cans

The key for maximizing aluminum value: remove steel screws, bolts, and hardware from aluminum window frames and other assemblies before going to the yard. A frame with steel attachments grades as mixed/dirty; remove the steel and you have clean aluminum.

## Steel: Lower Per-Pound Value, High Volume Potential

### What It Looks Like

Steel is magnetic and often heavy. It can be shiny (stainless varieties) or have a dark gray/rust finish (carbon steel). Rebar, I-beams, old appliance frames, bed frames, and tool bodies are typically steel.

### Where You Find It

- Bed frames and furniture frames
- Old appliances (washers, dryers, ranges — the structural steel)
- Rebar and construction steel from renovations
- Old auto parts
- Steel shelving and storage racks
- Cast iron from old cookware, radiators, engine blocks

### What It Pays

Steel pays significantly less per pound than copper or aluminum. However, steel items are often available in large quantities — a single old washer and dryer set, a steel bed frame, and a set of steel shelving can add up to several hundred pounds. At scale, steel cleanup makes sense even at lower per-pound prices.

Cast iron pays somewhat better than light steel because of its density and purity.

## Preparing Your Scrap for the Yard

A few practical tips to maximize your return:

- **Separate metals by type.** Mixed loads are always priced at the lowest grade in the mix.
- **Remove non-metal attachments** — rubber hoses, plastic fittings, wood blocks, fabric, and wire connections bring down the grade.
- **Call ahead** about requirements, minimums, and today's prices before making the trip.
- **Bring a valid ID.** Colorado scrap yards are required to collect and record seller information as part of anti-theft regulations.
- **Weigh it yourself first** if possible. A bathroom scale gives you a rough idea of what to expect.
