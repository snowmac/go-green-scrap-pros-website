If you've seen an ad for free scrap metal pickup and wondered whether there's a catch — it's a fair question. The honest answer is that free scrap pickup is real and it works, but it has limits. Understanding the model helps you know when it applies to your situation and when a standard junk removal service makes more sense.

## Why Free Scrap Pickup Exists

Scrap metal has market value. Steel, copper, aluminum, brass, and iron are commodities that recycling facilities buy by the pound. When a hauler picks up a load of scrap metal, they recover that cost — and often a profit — by selling the material to a metal recycler.

That's the core of how free pickup works: the metal itself pays for the service. The hauler earns from recycling, you get the metal gone at no cost.

## What Qualifies for Free Pickup

Not every piece of metal qualifies. Free pickup makes economic sense when the load is primarily metal with enough recoverable value to cover the time, labor, and transport involved.

**Items that commonly qualify:**

- Old appliances — washing machines, dryers, dishwashers, metal water heaters (not refrigerators/AC units, which have refrigerant that adds handling cost)
- Metal shelving units and racks
- Scrap steel, iron, or aluminum from construction or projects
- Old tools and equipment with metal components
- Bicycles and metal furniture frames
- Copper pipe and wire
- Steel I-beams, rebar, or structural metal from renovations

**Items that may complicate a free pickup:**

- Refrigerators, freezers, and AC units — these contain refrigerant that must be properly recovered before the metal can be scrapped. This adds cost to the job, so refrigerant-containing appliances are sometimes subject to a fee even if the metal itself has value.
- Mixed loads where metal is a small portion of a larger haul of non-metal junk

## When There's a Charge

Free scrap metal pickup has a threshold. If you have one appliance surrounded by piles of wood, drywall, mattresses, and general junk, the overall load likely won't be free — the non-metal material has disposal costs that the metal value doesn't offset.

In that case, you're looking at standard junk removal pricing, which is based on load size and material type. That's not a bait-and-switch — it's just math. A load that's 80% recyclable metal is a different job than a mixed load with a few metal pieces.

## What Happens to the Metal

Every load we pick up goes to licensed scrap metal recyclers. Ferrous metals (steel and iron) and non-ferrous metals (copper, aluminum, brass) are processed separately because they have different market values and recycling pathways.

Recycled metal re-enters the manufacturing stream — it becomes new steel beams, aluminum cans, copper wire, or whatever the market needs. It's genuinely circular recycling, not a feel-good label.
