If you've seen an ad for free scrap metal pickup and wondered whether there's a catch — it's a fair question. The honest answer: some loads are free, most aren't, and which one you get depends on what's actually in the load. Here's exactly how it works.

## Why Some Loads Are Free and Others Aren't

Scrap metal has market value, but not all scrap has *enough* value to cover the cost of a dedicated pickup. Commercial HVAC units, server racks, and clean copper or brass batches carry enough recycling value that the metal itself pays for the trip — those often qualify for free pickup. Everything else — old appliances, steel, mixed scrap, general household metal — still gets picked up, just for a flat dispatch fee based on distance, since the recycling value on its own doesn't cover the time and fuel.

## What Qualifies for Free Pickup

Free pickup is the exception, not the default. It's reserved for high-value categories:

- **Commercial HVAC units and ductwork**
- **Server racks and commercial IT equipment**
- **Clean copper pipe, tubing, or wire — in batch quantities**
- **Clean brass fittings and valves — in batch quantities**

We confirm eligibility from a photo before scheduling anything, so there's no guesswork on either side.

**Everything else gets a dispatch fee, not free pickup:**

- Old appliances — washers, dryers, dishwashers, water heaters
- Refrigerators and freezers (Freon recovery included)
- Metal shelving, racks, and fencing
- Steel, iron, aluminum, or mixed scrap from construction or projects
- Old tools and equipment with metal components
- General household metal — bed frames, bikes, filing cabinets, and similar

## How Much Is the Dispatch Fee

The fee is flat and distance-based from our Thornton hub:

## What Happens to the Metal

Every load we pick up goes to licensed scrap metal recyclers. Ferrous metals (steel and iron) and non-ferrous metals (copper, aluminum, brass) are processed separately because they have different market values and recycling pathways.

Recycled metal re-enters the manufacturing stream — it becomes new steel beams, aluminum cans, copper wire, or whatever the market needs. It's genuinely circular recycling, not a feel-good label.
